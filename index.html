THE   FORMATION   OF   THE   UNIVERSE

The big bang model is the most widely accepted theory regarding the formation of the universe. It was first proposed in the 1920s by Belgian astronomer Georges Lemaître and Russian mathematician Aleksandr Friedmann. In the 1940s George Gamow and his colleagues developed the modern version of this theory which we still use today. The theory assumes that Albert Einstein’s general theory of relativity and the cosmological principle are true. This theory applies only to the large-scale aspects of the universe because Einstein’s Theory of Relativity does not apply in the quantum space. The big bang model states that the universe doesn’t have an edge which means that the big bang didn’t happen at one point in space but throughout space at the same time. The universe was very dense at the beginning and had a lot of heat energy. The big bang wasn't an explosion it was the rapid expansion of the universe and soon after the beginning of the expansion matter asserted dominance over antimatter. In this stage, there may have been many elementary particles present. A few seconds after that the universe had cooled down enough to allow the formation of some nuclei and certain amounts of hydrogen, helium, and lithium were produced. And finally, about 1 million years later the universe had cooled down enough so that atoms could form.
